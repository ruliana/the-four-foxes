# Case Design

Use this reference when creating or revising cases, case trifolds, node lists, clues, suspects, or media-inspired scenario material.

## What a case leaflet is for

A case leaflet is a **brief descriptive node handout for the GM**.

It is not the plot graph itself.
Do **not** explain how the nodes connect unless the user explicitly asks for that.
The leaflet should give the GM evocative material to connect during play.

## Default jobs of case content

Case material in this project should usually provide:

- a strong case hook
- a short premise
- a few events
- a few powers or long-range forces
- brief node lists for people, places, and objects
- optionally, alternative seeds showing how different starting nodes create different cases

## Node writing rules

Keep nodes brief and usable.
Favor description over explanation.

### People

People should emphasize:
- appearance first
- presence, silhouette, and immediate read
- sometimes a hint of what they do or how they move through the world

Good:
- what they look like
- what kind of room they belong in
- one strong behavioral read

Avoid:
- long biographies
- hidden backstory dumps
- explicit connection mapping

### Places

Places should emphasize:
- tone
- atmosphere
- sensory read
- the social or emotional temperature of the location

Good:
- heat, smoke, rain, dust, echoes, light, crowd pressure
- whether a place feels public, intimate, exposed, defensive, decayed, false-safe, or watched

Avoid:
- tactical floorplan detail unless the user wants it
- long functional explanation

### Objects

Objects should emphasize:
- what they are
- what they look and feel like
- what makes them visually or materially distinct

Good:
- shape, weight, finish, script, age, damage, material, ornament

Avoid by default:
- explaining what the object does
- explaining what it is for
- explaining why everyone wants it

### Events

Events should be short snapshots of something that happened, is happening, or is about to happen.

Good event nodes usually feel like:
- a charged situation
- a public incident
- a discovery
- a transfer
- a ritual opening
- a death, theft, escape, or interruption

Avoid:
- full chronology
- causal explanation trees
- scene-by-scene summaries of the source material

### Powers

Powers should be written in terms of **goals**.

Good power nodes answer:
- what long-range force is in play?
- what does it want?
- why does it persist beyond one case?

Avoid:
- too much lore
- too many named sub-factions unless necessary

## Case leaflets inspired by existing media

When adapting a film, novel, comic, myth, or other source:

- omit original character names unless the user explicitly wants them
- capture the **case energy**, not the exact plot
- keep what is useful for play: motives, locations, objects, tensions, event types, and mood
- replace source elements that are memorable in the film but weak as playable nodes
- feel free to diverge hard if the case works better that way

The goal is not faithful retelling.
The goal is a **good Four Foxes case** that draws inspiration from the source.

## What to replace when adapting

Prefer replacing source elements that are:
- iconic on screen but weak in play
- redundant with stronger nodes already present
- geographically distant without adding new play value
- visually memorable but structurally irrelevant

A replacement should usually be:
- easier to use at the table
- easier to connect to multiple other nodes
- stronger in tone or pressure
- more useful for pursuit, investigation, negotiation, or escape

## Seeds and divergence

A strong case can support several very different seeds.

When useful, add an **Other Seeds** section to demonstrate that:
- the same material can begin from different node combinations
- one seed may resemble the source inspiration
- others may produce very different cases

This is especially useful when the case is inspired by a well-known film, because it reminds the reader that the graph procedure matters more than the source plot.

### Good seed examples should

- name exactly three starting nodes
- give a one- or two-sentence description of the kind of case that seed produces
- make the differences between seeds feel real, not cosmetic

At least one alternative seed should create a substantially different case shape.

## Tone for Four Foxes cases

Default tone should feel like some mixture of:
- pulp adventure
- hard-boiled investigation
- occult dread
- old institutions under strain
- material reality pressed against myth

Even when the case is adventurous, keep one foot in unease.

## Case leaflet layout guidance

For compact trifolds, good panel splits include:

### Split A
- cover
- premise / tone / events / powers
- other seeds
- people
- places
- objects

### Split B
- cover
- premise / tone / powers
- events
- people
- places
- objects

Choose the split that scans best at print size.

## Styling guidance for case nodes

For case leaflets, default to **elegant period presentation** over boxed UI.

Prefer:
- small caps or display headings
- thin rules
- open spacing
- restrained ornament
- text that feels like a 1920s printed handout

Avoid by default:
- heavy boxes around every node
- modern card-grid styling
- dense UI-like containers

Boxes may still be appropriate for diagrams, examples, or special callouts — just not as the default node style.

## Workflow for creating or revising a case leaflet

1. Identify the playable core of the case.
2. Decide whether the case is original or inspired by existing media.
3. If inspired, strip away source-specific names unless the user wants them.
4. List candidate nodes under People, Places, Objects, Events, and Powers.
5. Cut weak or redundant nodes.
6. Rewrite each node to match its proper style:
   - People = appearance and presence
   - Places = tone and atmosphere
   - Objects = what it is
   - Events = charged snapshot
   - Powers = goal
7. If the adaptation comes from a known source, consider adding **Other Seeds** with one source-adjacent seed and at least one divergent seed.
8. If implementing in Typst, keep the layout printer-friendly and easy to scan.
9. Compile and preview both pages.

## Final checks for cases

Before finishing, confirm:
- the leaflet is not secretly explaining the connection map
- the nodes feel brief and playable
- the events feel like strong pressure points
- the powers are goal-driven
- the case works even if it diverges from its inspiration
- any alternative seeds produce meaningfully different cases
- the document still scans quickly when rendered
